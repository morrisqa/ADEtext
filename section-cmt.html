Section 1.3 Contraction Mapping Theorem

Suppose you are given the task of finding all solution to the vector equation

\begin{equation} \left[ \begin{array}{c} x \\ y \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c} \frac{1}{2} \sin (y) \\ \frac{1}{2} \cos(x) \end{array} \right].\tag{1.3.1} \end{equation}

You might fumble around for a bit, searching for a method of solving these, attempting to solve for one of the variables and then substituting in to find the other. Indeed, this is the usual method of solving a system (particularly a linear system, which this is not) presented in many math courses. However, in this case, we will take a slightly different approach.

Consider the function \(T:\mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2\) which is defined by

\begin{equation*} T \left(\left[ \begin{array}{c} x \\ y \end{array} \right] \right) = \left[ \begin{array}{c} \frac{1}{2} \sin (y) \\ \frac{1}{2} \cos(x) \end{array} \right]. \end{equation*}

Note that solutions to our original equation are now points which satisfy

\begin{equation*} \left[ \begin{array}{c} x \\ y \end{array} \right] = T \left(\left[ \begin{array}{c} x \\ y \end{array} \right]\right). \end{equation*}

Such a point is called a fixed point of the map \(T\text{,}\) and the main focus of this section will be to find fixed points of maps which correspond to solutions of equations (for now, a vector equation, and then later, a differential equation) which we're interested in.

Definition 1.3.1.

Let \(X\) be a set and let \(T:X \to X\) be a function. A point \(x \in X\) is called a fixed point of \(T\) if \(T(x)=x\text{.}\)

In this case, we'd like to try to identify a fixed point of our map \(T\text{,}\) as that would be equivalent to finding a solution to (1.3.1). Finding a fixed point, however, still remains a challenge. First, we may observe that if \(\left[ \begin{array}{c} x \\ y \end{array} \right]\) is a fixed point, then \(-\frac{1}{2}\le x \le \frac{1}{2} \) and \(-\frac{1}{2}\le y \le \frac{1}{2} \) since \(x=\frac{1}{2}\sin(y)\) and \(y=\frac{1}{2}\cos(x)\text{.}\) So, let's look at what happens to all vectors in the set

\begin{equation*} \left\{\left[ \begin{array}{c} x \\ y \end{array} \right] \mid -\frac{1}{2}\le x \le \frac{1}{2} \text{ and } -\frac{1}{2}\le y \le \frac{1}{2} \right\}. \end{equation*}

Example 1.3.2.

Show that the differential equation

\begin{equation*} \left\{ \begin{array}{l} y''=y \\ y(0)=0 \\ y'(0)=3 \end{array} \right. \end{equation*}

has a solution.

Solution.

Since the differential equation \(y''=y\) is linear with constant coefficients, we could consider its characteristic equation, find the roots, and use those to write down a solution. The initial conditions would then be used to find unknown constants.

For this problem, however, such a method is likely unnecessary. We may observe that we know two linearly independent functions, namely \(y_1(x)=\sin(x)\) and \(y_2(x)=\cos(x)\text{,}\) which satisfy \(y''=y\text{.}\) Hence, any solution must be of the form \(y(t)=c_1y_1(t) + c_2y_2(t)\text{,}\) and the initial conditions are again used to find the unknown constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\text{.}\)

In this case, the reader should find that \(y(t)=3\sin(t)\) is a solution. What is harder to show is that this is the only solution.
