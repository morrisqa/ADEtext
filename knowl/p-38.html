It is clear that \(z(t)=0\) is a solution for all \(t \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Assume there is another solution, say \(w(t)\text{.}\) Using the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, \(z(t)\) and \(w(t)\) may be written as

\begin{equation*} z(t)=z(0)-\int_0^t |z(s)| \; ds = \int_0^t |z(s)| \; ds \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} w(t)=w(0)-\int_0^t |w(s)| \; ds = \int_0^t |w(s)| \; ds, \end{equation*}

respectively, for all \(t \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then,

\begin{equation*} |w(t)-z(t)|= \left| \int_0^t |w(s)| \; ds - \int_0^t |z(s)| \; ds \right| = \left| \int_0^t |w(s)| - |z(s)| \; ds \right| \; \; \; \forall t \in \mathbb{R}. \end{equation*}

and, utilizing the fact that \(\left|\int_a^b g(s)\; ds \right| \le \int_a^b |g(s)| \; ds\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} |w(t)-z(t)| = \left| \int_0^t |w(s)| - |z(s)| \; ds \right| \le \int_0^t \left| |w(s)| - |z(s)| \right| \; ds. \end{equation*}

Finally, we apply the reverse triangle inequality to observe that

\begin{equation*} |w(t)-z(t)| \le \int_0^t \left| |w(s)| - |z(s)| \right| \; ds \le \int_0^t \left| w(s) - z(s) \right| \; ds. \end{equation*}

Now, since this inequality holds for all \(t\in\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) we may apply Gronwall's Inequality with \(f(t)=|w(t)-z(t)|\text{,}\) \(A=0\text{,}\) and \(B=1\text{.}\) Hence, \(|w(t)-z(t)|\le 0\cdot e^{1(t-0)}=0\text{,}\) and since \(|w(t)-z(t)|\ge 0\) by definition of the absolute value function, we must have \(|w(t)-z(t)|=0\) for all \(t \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) This implies that \(w(t)=z(t)\) for all \(t \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) and hence we only have one solution.
