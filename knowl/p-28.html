As ExampleÂ 1.1.2 showed, continuity of \(\vec{f}\) is not enough to guarantee the existence of a unique solution. It's worth recalling the problem here, which was,

\begin{equation*} \left\{ \begin{array}{l} y'=y^{\frac{2}{3}} \\ y(0)=0 \end{array} \right. \end{equation*}

Clearly we need to seek out a more stringent criteria for \(\vec{f}\) if we want to ensure that the initial value problem has a unique solution. Our natural intuition from calculus may suggest to us that we try to establish an existence and uniqueness theorem when \(\vec{f}\) is differentiable. This is quite a reasonable hypothesis, particularly since we note that the failure of uniqueness occurs when the initial value \(y(0)=0\) occurs where the function \(f(y)=y^\frac{2}{3}\) fails to be differentiable.
